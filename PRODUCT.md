# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Small IT teams (1-3 people) at startups and SMBs. Often a DevOps engineer or "accidental CISO" who is also responsible for security. They are not dedicated security specialists: they know enough to be worried, not enough to triage 400 alerts. They have infrastructure to protect, limited time, and no budget for a full security team.

Secondary: security-curious founders or CTOs at early-stage companies who want automated coverage without hiring.

## Product Purpose

Horus automates the security pipeline that small teams can't staff: discover your attack surface, scan for vulnerabilities, correlate findings against live threat intelligence, debate ambiguous results with adversarial AI agents, and surface only what needs your attention. Configure once; agents run every night without anyone on-call. The product exists so a team of 2 can have the coverage of a 5-person security function.

Success: the user wakes up to 1-3 things that actually need action instead of 400 unread alerts.

## Brand Personality

Innovative, agile, autonomous. The brand speaks like the tool: direct, no filler, confident in what it does. It earns trust through specificity (real CVE IDs, real EPSS scores, real terminal output) not through enterprise-polish promises.

## Anti-references

- HackerOne / Synack: offensive-security hacker aesthetic, green neon, bug bounty culture. Wrong register: Horus is defensive (blue team) and serves operators, not hunters.
- CrowdStrike / Palo Alto: glossy enterprise SaaS with stock photography, vague promises of "full protection", blue-navy color systems. Too heavy for SMB buyers who want something deployable by one person.
- Wiz / Orca: cloud-only, pastel-minimal, "shift-left" messaging. Too cloud-specific; Horus covers on-prem, hybrid, and cloud stacks.

## Design Principles

1. **Show the output, not the feature.** A terminal with a real CVE match beats a feature list. Demonstrate what the user's morning inbox looks like.
2. **Specificity builds trust.** CVE IDs, EPSS scores, real command names, actual framework names. Vague superlatives erode credibility with technical buyers.
3. **Reduce, then alert.** Every copy and UX decision should reinforce the core promise: fewer things reaching the human, but the ones that do truly matter.
4. **Open-source as differentiator, not afterthought.** MIT license and self-host aren't "also available" features; they are the reason a startup trusts this over an enterprise vendor.
5. **One engineer's confidence.** The audience is a single person protecting a company. The product should feel like a force-multiplier, not a platform that requires a team to operate.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Dark theme must maintain ≥4.5:1 body text contrast. Reduced motion honored (currently implemented in CSS). No reliance on color alone for severity levels.
